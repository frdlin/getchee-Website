A

Accessibility
An aggregate measure of the degree of ease with which a place, person, or thing can be reached, depending on factors such as slope, traffic, distance, and so on.

Analog Store
A store that is similar or closesly resembles another store location in certain respects. This could be due to its similarity in demography, retail mix, phisical site location, and/or other geographic attributes.

B

Base Map
A map containing essential geographic features used for locational reference. Roads, for example, are commonly found on base maps.

C

Cannibalization
A calculation of the amount of overlap between two or more service areas (sometimes known as trade areas).

Coordinates
A set of numbers, or a single number, that locates a point on a line, on a plane, or in space.

Customized & Value-added Datasets
Datasets created by getchee that highlight a segment of the population different from the standard like affluent and employable population segments.

D

Data Aggregator
An organization involved in compiling information from detailed databases on individuals and selling that information to others.

Demographic(s)
Demographics or demographic data refers to selected population characteristics as used in government, marketing or opinion research, or the demographic profiles used in such research.

Demographic Profile
A term used in marketing and broadcasting, to describe a demographic grouping or a market segment.

E

Estimated Market Potential
An indication of market opportunity using a derivative of target consumer segments, competition, and cannibalization analysis.

F

Feature Data
Data that represents geographic features as geometric shapes.

G

Gap Analysis
A tool that helps a company to compare its actual performance with its potential performance.

Geocoding
The process of finding associated geographic coordinates (often expressed as latitude and longitude) from other geographic data, such as street addresses, or zip codes (postal codes).

Geographic Information System (GIS)
A system that captures, stores, analyzes, manages, and presents data that is linked to location.

Grid
In cartography, any network of parallel and perpendicular lines superimposed on a map and used for reference.

H

Household Number
The number of families, or houses, in a given geographic location.

L

L-commerce
The practice of using technologies which provide location information, such as GPS, for business purposes. An example of l-commerce would be to allow cellphone users to find the nearest restaurant to their current location.

M

Map Extent
The limit of the geographic area shown on a map, usually defined by a rectangle. In a dynamic map display, the map extent can be changed by zooming and panning.

Map Point
A point on a map with specific X and Y coordinates. It is usually marked by a drop pin.

N

Non-spatial Data
Data without inherently spatial qualities, such as attributes.

P

Point of Interest (POI)
A point of interest, or POI, is a specific point location that someone may find useful or interesting. Most consumers use the term when referring to hotels, restaurants, fuel stations or any other categories used in modern mapping and navigation systems.

S

Service Area
The area, typically around a store, from which it derives most of its patronage from. This is also known or refered to as a trade area.

Scale
The proportion or ratio between a map measurement and the corresponding measurement in the real world. Map scale is usually expressed as a ratio, such as 1:24,000, which means that a measurement of one unit on the map represents 24,000 units in the real world.

Study Area
Study Areas are geographic boundaries created to define the extent of your analysis. These are created when starting a project to ensure your data and analyses are confined to a specified area.

T

Trade Area
The area, typically around a store, from which it derives most of its patronage from. This is also known or refered to as a service area.

Trade Zone
A geographic area that contains a high density of retail infrastructure. They are generally where people go for entertainment (food, shopping, etc.), but can also be places where people work.

W

Working Population

X

X,Y Coordinates
A pair of values that represents the distance from an origin (0,0) along two axes, a horizontal axis (x), and a vertical axis (y). On a map, x,y coordinates are used to represent features at the location they are found on the earth's spherical surface.

X-axis
In a planar coordinate system, the horizontal line that runs right and left (east and west of) the origin (0,0).

Y

Y-axis
In a planar coordinate system, the vertical line that runs above and below (north and south of) the origin (0,0). Numbers north of the origin are positive, and numbers south of it are negative.

Z

Z-axis
In a spherical coordinate system, the vertical line that runs parallel to the earth's rotation, passing through 90 degrees north latitude, and perpendicular to the equatorial plane, where it crosses the x- and y-axes at the origin (0,0,0).
